## Phase 1 — Learn (40 min)

**Why this matters:** You could build the best design in this course and still not get hired if you can't explain your process. A portfolio case study is not a gallery of beautiful screenshots — it's evidence of your design thinking. It shows: you understood the problem, you talked to users, you made decisions based on data, you iterated, and you can articulate why each decision was the right one. That's what gets you hired.

- **Case Study Structure:**
  1. *Problem* — what problem were you solving and for whom? One clear sentence. "Users of [app type] struggle to [specific pain point] because [root cause]."
  2. *Research* — what did you learn and how? User interviews, competitive analysis, journey mapping. Highlight the most surprising or insight-changing finding.
  3. *Process* — what did you try? Show iterations. Show a wireframe that failed. Show what the usability test revealed. This is where most candidates fail — they skip straight to the solution.
  4. *Solution* — what did you build? Show the hi-fi screens, the prototype link. Explain the key design decisions and why you made them.
  5. *Outcome* — what changed? If you tested it: "5-user test showed task completion improved from 3/5 to 5/5 on the core task." If it's a course project: "I would measure [metric]. The next iteration would address [specific unresolved finding]."
- **What NOT to do:**
  - Don't just show final screens. Show the before and after. Show sketches and wireframes.
  - Don't use design jargon without explaining it. "I leveraged affordances to optimise discoverability" means nothing to most readers.
  - Don't make it too long. A focused 500-word case study with 6 well-chosen images is better than a 2,000-word gallery.
  - Don't hide failures. A failed iteration you fixed is evidence of good process. Pretending everything worked perfectly is unconvincing.
- **What to include:**
  - The problem statement (1–2 sentences)
  - At least one research artefact (a persona, a key insight quote, a journey map highlight)
  - One wireframe or early iteration
  - One hi-fi screen or prototype screenshot
  - One usability finding and how you responded to it
  - The prototype link (always include a live link)
  - What you'd do next (shows forward-thinking)

> **Key insight:** Your case study is an argument. You're arguing: "I am the kind of designer who solves the right problems in the right way." Every section of the case study is evidence for that argument. If a piece of content doesn't support the argument, cut it.

## Phase 2 — Do (50 min)

Write the case study for your course project:
1. **Problem statement** — one paragraph: what app were you redesigning or designing, who are the users, what specific problem did you find in your research?
2. **Research section** — include:
   - The persona you created in Session 02 (or a summary)
   - One key insight from your journey map: "The biggest drop in user satisfaction was at [stage] because [pain point]"
   - One competitor gap you identified
3. **Process section** — include:
   - One lo-fi wireframe (from Session 03/04) with a brief explanation of the structural decisions
   - One thing the usability test (Session 04 or 08) revealed that surprised you
   - How you changed the design in response
4. **Solution section** — include:
   - 2–3 hi-fi screens from Session 07
   - Brief rationale for 2 key design decisions: "We placed the [element] at [location] because [user behaviour/research insight]"
   - The prototype link
5. **Outcome section** — write 2–3 sentences about what you measured or would measure, and what you'd work on next
6. Format the case study either in Figma (design it as a PDF/presentation) or in a Google Doc with embedded images — whichever format you'll use in your portfolio.

## Review

Look back at your case study draft. Does every section answer a question a hiring manager or client would ask? What question does the Problem section answer? What question does the Process section answer?
